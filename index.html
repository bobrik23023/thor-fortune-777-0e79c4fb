How the peg pyramid works

The board is a staggered triangle of pegs, with each row one peg wider than the row above it. A ball dropped from the top has an equal chance of moving left or right at every peg, repeating that split on the way down. Once it clears the last row, it lands in one of the bottom pockets — always one more pocket than the number of rows.

Because every path is built from repeated fifty-fifty choices, balls tend to collect near the centre while edge pockets remain far less common. Statisticians call this a Galton board, and the pattern it creates is a binomial distribution. The paytable is that curve turned upside down: common middle pockets pay less, while rare outer pockets pay more.
